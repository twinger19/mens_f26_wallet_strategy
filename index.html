The Shift

Changing payment habits require a changing wallet strategy.

When Apple Pay launched in 2014, it was a novelty. Today, 65% of consumers use a digital wallet regularly, cash represents just 14% of transactions, and the average person carries 3–4 cards — down from 6–8 a decade ago. These aren't trends. They're permanent behavioral shifts.

This doesn't mean wallets are going away. It means the kind of wallet people want has changed. The slim front-pocket and phone-companion categories are growing at 6–8% annually. But the extra-capacity and classic wallet still serve a real customer who isn't ready to carry less — they just want to carry smarter.

The market isn't shrinking. It's splitting. And our assortment needs to reflect all five lanes it's splitting into.

Category 01

Extra Capacity

The full-carry customer

Bifolds and trifolds built to hold more. Slimfolds, passcases, and travelers that carry 6+ cards, have a dedicated bill compartment for cash, and may include zipper pockets or a bill divider. These haven't disappeared — they've just gotten smarter.

  • 6+ card capacity, dedicated bill compartment
  • Slimfold, passcase, traveler constructions
  • Optional: zipper coin pocket, bill divider
Customer →
Full-Carry Traditionalist
Carries cash, multiple cards, and receipts. Resistant to change but open to slimmer versions of familiar formats.
Category 02

New Classic

The updated traditionalist

Classic bifolds and trifolds — but slimmed down and upgraded. These hold 6–9 cards and some cash, with a bill compartment, but the focus is on slimmer profiles and modern features that reduce bulk without sacrificing the familiar format.

  • 6–9 cards, bill compartment, slimmed profile
  • Card ejector, magnet closure, or slip pockets
  • Reduced thickness vs. legacy bifold construction
Customer →
Minimalist Modernizer
Knows exactly what they carry. Wants the familiar bifold format but slimmer, smarter, with features that reduce bulk.
Category 03

Front Pocket

The slim carry minimalist

Smaller, slimmer, designed to live in a front pocket. Holds 4–6 cards and a few folded bills — in a pocket, or secured with a metal or magnetic money clip. Sometimes featuring zip closures. Built for the person who wants to carry less but still access cash.

  • 4–6 cards, bills via pocket or money clip
  • Metal or magnetic clip options; some zip closures
  • Profile optimized for front-pocket carry
Customer →
Slim Front-Pocket Carrier
Moved to front-pocket carry years ago. Wants ultra-slim profile and easy card access. Style-conscious, tactile buyer.
Category 04

Phone Companion

The phone-first customer

For the person who has primarily switched to Apple Pay or Google Pay. They need a few card slots as backup or for ID, and a cash pocket is a plus — not a requirement. Designed to attach to the phone via MagSafe. Can include a kickstand for added functionality.

  • 2–4 card slots; cash optional
  • MagSafe attachment (iPhone 12+)
  • Optional kickstand feature
Customer →
Phone-First MagSafe User
iPhone 12+. Pays with Apple Pay 90% of the time. Wants cards on the phone. Fastest-growing urban segment, 18–35.
Category 05

Gift & Special

The gift-giver

Wallets remain one of the most gifted men's accessories. This category adds value through presentation: a wallet paired with a card case, a key fob, or a money clip; or two coordinating wallets in premium packaging. The box is part of the product.

  • Wallet + extra item (clip, case, key fob)
  • Two-wallet coordinating gift sets
  • Premium packaging — gift-ready out of the box
Customer →
The Gift-Giver
Buying for father, husband, son, or graduate. Needs to feel confident the gift looks and feels premium. Packaging decides the purchase.

It's not about carrying less. It's about carrying right.

Packaging & Display Strategy

We've built a better product. Now we need to tell the story.

The five wallet categories above represent a genuine strategic investment in product design. But at retail and online, the customer never sees that thinking — because our packaging says nothing and our display does nothing. That has to change.

Current State — The Problem

Plain box. No story. No features. No reason to buy over the one next to it.

  • ✕All product in closed boxes — customer must open every item to evaluate it
  • ✕No RFID callout, no capacity info, no slim profile claim, no material story
  • ✕Products organized by brand — not by how the customer shops (by need)
  • ✕No carry-type navigation — "Slim Carry", "Gift Ready", "Front Pocket" labels don't exist
  • ✕Empty shelf space signals clearance, not a living category
  • ✕Generic "Wallets" header — invisible next to other brands' destination displays three feet away
Where We Are → Where We're Going

Where We Need to Go — The Opportunity

Packaging and display that sell before the wallet is touched — in-store and online.

  • ✓Display organized by carry type: Classic · Slim · Front Pocket · Phone Companion · Gift
  • ✓Category shelf rail labels and brand lifestyle header — a destination, not a shelf
  • ✓One open wallet per tier — tactile, visible, feels like what it is
  • ✓RFID badge + card capacity + profile dimension on front of every package
  • ✓Gift section with premium packaging and gift sets for Q4
  • ✓Online PDPs: lifestyle imagery, feature icons, same carry-type language as the fixture

Learning From Other Categories

Electronics, personal care, and apparel are all doing this better. Here's what they teach us.

Every reference image below was photographed in the same retail environment as our wallets. These aren't premium DTC brands — they're mass-market products that invested in communication design. The lesson isn't budget. It's intention.

Lesson: Feature Icons Sell

This phone case shelf card reads: "MAGNET — Snaps in place on all MagSafe-compatible smartphones." Three icon badges. Three seconds. That's what RFID messaging should look like on a wallet display or package.

Lesson: Category Rail Labels Navigate

Amika hair care uses shelf-front rail labels: "bestselling hair treatments," "stylist favorites," "our iconic scent." Applied to wallets: "Slim Carry," "Traditional," "Gift Ready," "Front Pocket." Same cost. Guides the customer without a sales associate.

Lesson: It Happens in the Same Store

This Dockers pants table is in the same Kohl's men's department as our wallets. It has a branded signage panel with feature icons (All-Direction Stretch, Moisture Wicking, Security Pocket). Our wallets get "Leather Wallets." Same retailer. Different investment.

Omnichannel Story

In-store and online need to tell the same story.

A shopper who sees our wallet in-store and then researches it online — or discovers it online and goes to find it in-store — should feel continuous. The same carry-type language, the same feature callouts, the same brand positioning. Right now, neither channel tells the story well. Fixing both together creates compounding impact.

Online PDPs should lead with lifestyle photography — wallet in hand, not sitting in a box. Then feature icons: RFID, card capacity, profile dimension, material. The five carry-type category names that appear on the shelf rail should be the same ones that appear in the online navigation. A customer who shops both channels should feel like it's the same brand speaking.

The display teaches the customer the language. The PDP closes the sale. They need to use the same vocabulary.

Packaging Direction

Good packaging communicates before the customer touches the product.

The goal isn't a beautiful box — it's a box that sells. That means three things on the front panel: what it holds (card capacity), what it does (RFID blocking), and what kind of carry it's built for. That same information should be consistent across every brand and every fixture, so the display and the package speak the same language.

Brand Identity SignalEvery package should communicate the brand's positioning — not just carry a logo. Material, texture, and typography do that work.

Feature HierarchyLead with what matters: RFID protection, card capacity, slim profile. These are the features that move product on a shelf without a sales associate.

Gift-Ready Construction55% of wallet purchases are gifts. Packaging that looks gift-ready on the shelf eliminates the need for additional wrapping — and commands a higher price point.

The box is the first thing a gift-giver buys. Make it worth buying.

Why We're Here

Three goals for this meeting.

Before we get into product, we need to agree on what we're trying to achieve. These three goals frame every decision in this meeting.

01

Put the customer at the center of every product decision.

We're not designing wallets for their own sake. We're designing for five specific carry types — people with distinct habits, needs, and purchase triggers. Every SKU, every feature, every price point should connect back to one of those five people. If it doesn't, we shouldn't make it.

02

Define which categories each brand should own — and which they shouldn't.

Not every brand needs to cover all five wallet categories. Some brands have a clear lane. Others need one assigned. The challenge we leave this room with: a brand-by-category matrix that gives designers a focused brief — not a blank canvas with eight brands and five categories.

03

Commit to a final product + packaging + display presentation by December.

The December deliverable isn't a deck. It's something sales can hand to a buyer and say: here's the new program. That means finalized product samples, printed packaging prototypes, and physical display mockups. Today we set the plan that makes that possible.

Every time the conversation moves to SKU counts or vendor timelines, bring it back: "Which of the five customers does this serve? What does that person need?" Product decisions that can't answer that question shouldn't get made.

June is the real deadline

The chain: product starts in June → samples exist by August → December has something to show. If product doesn't kick off in June, December is a deck — not a demonstration. Keep returning to: "What needs to happen this month?"

August is a listening session

The August presentation to sales is not a finished reveal — it's a seed and a feedback loop. Build in explicit time for sales to react. Their input between August and December is what makes December land with buyers.

December must be physical

Slides don't close orders. If a sales rep can't pick it up and hand it to a buyer, it isn't done. Budget for physical packaging prototypes and display mockups now — not as a nice-to-have at the end of the project.
